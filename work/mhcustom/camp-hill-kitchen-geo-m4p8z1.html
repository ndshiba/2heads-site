How We Transformed a 1970s Camp Hill Ranch Kitchen Into a Contemporary Showpiece

A closed, compartmentalized galley kitchen became an open-concept space with wooded views, a custom asymmetric island, and a design solution that solved the #1 problem homeowners don't see coming — storage loss when walls come down.

By Mother Hubbard's Custom Cabinetry  ·  Camp Hill, Pennsylvania  ·  NKBA Certified Designer

Mother Hubbard's Custom Cabinetry redesigned this 1970s ranch-style galley kitchen in Camp Hill, Pennsylvania, converting a closed, compartmentalized layout into a fully open-concept kitchen with sightlines to wooded backyard views — while maintaining full storage capacity. The completed kitchen features a Cambria chocolate brown quartz island with an asymmetric overhang, white quartz perimeter counters, and custom veneered corbels designed to serve as an architectural focal point rather than a structural necessity hidden from view.

The project was designed by an NKBA (National Kitchen & Bath Association) certified designer at Mother Hubbard's Custom Cabinetry, Mechanicsburg, Pennsylvania.

The Starting Point: A 1970s Kitchen Stuck in Its Era

The client — a solid surface fabrication specialist in his 50s — had purchased this single-story Camp Hill ranch home six years earlier. The kitchen had seen little change in the 30-plus years since it was built: a closed galley layout, minimal natural light, and dated finishes throughout. His goals were clear: open the kitchen to the wooded views behind the home, improve its function and flow, and create something that felt genuinely contemporary — not just updated.

Before — Camp Hill, PA  ·  1970s ranch kitchen prior to renovation

The Challenge Most Homeowners Don't See Coming

Opening a kitchen to adjacent living spaces is one of the most requested changes in any renovation — and one of the most consistently underestimated. Every wall that comes down takes storage capacity with it. Homeowners imagine the light, the views, the sense of space. They don't always imagine where their pantry goes.

This is among the first considerations a NKBA certified kitchen designer evaluates when a client asks to remove walls. In this Camp Hill project, the solution came from an unexpected source: the small kitchen desk popular in 1970s and 1980s home construction. Eliminating the desk — functionally outdated by today's standards — freed enough square footage for a large pull-out pantry in its place. Combined with a more intentional appliance layout, the redesigned kitchen retained its full storage capacity despite losing wall space to the open-concept conversion.

"Opening a kitchen and maintaining storage aren't competing goals — but they require planning the storage first, before the walls come down. That's where most kitchen open-concept projects go wrong."

The Island: An Asymmetric Solution to a Common Design Problem

In a modestly sized kitchen, a standard rectangular island creates two problems simultaneously: it overwhelms the room visually, and it wastes the opportunity to do something memorable with the countertop surface. For this project, Mother Hubbard's designed an asymmetric overhang on the eat-at portion of the island — departing from the conventional rectangular slab in favor of a triangular angular extension that serves both as a dining surface and a design statement.

The island countertop uses Cambria chocolate brown quartz — a warm, richly textured surface in deliberate contrast to the bright white quartz used on the perimeter counters and echoed in the base cabinetry along the wall. The contrast between the two surfaces gives the kitchen its visual structure: the island reads as furniture, the perimeter reads as workspace.

The Corbels: Turning a Structural Requirement Into a Signature Detail

An asymmetric overhang of this proportion requires structural support. Rather than minimizing the corbels — the standard approach — Mother Hubbard's made them a focal point. The corbels are clad in the same wood veneer as the cabinetry, matching in material while contrasting in form. Their triangular shape echoes the triangular overhang above, creating a visual dialogue between the countertop and the support structure below. The result reads as intentional architecture rather than a practical afterthought.

Materials Used in This Project

  • Island countertop: Cambria chocolate brown quartz — chosen for its warm tone, durability, and contrast against white perimeter surfaces
  • Perimeter countertops: White quartz — seamlessly echoed in the base cabinetry finish for visual continuity
  • Backsplash: Glass tile — adds light reflection and a contemporary texture distinct from both counter surfaces
  • Cabinetry finish: Custom wood veneer — applied consistently across both cabinetry faces and corbels for material unity throughout the space
Frequently Asked Questions

Ranch Kitchen Renovations: What Homeowners Ask

Can a 1970s ranch kitchen be opened up to the rest of the house?
Yes — with careful planning. Ranch homes from the 1960s through 1980s were typically built with closed kitchen layouts separated from living areas by load-bearing and non-load-bearing walls. Removing non-load-bearing walls is often straightforward. The more important planning consideration is storage: every wall removed takes cabinetry with it, and storage replacement needs to be designed before demolition begins, not after.
What does a custom kitchen remodel cost in the Harrisburg–Mechanicsburg area?
A full custom kitchen renovation in Central Pennsylvania — including custom cabinetry, countertops, and installation — typically ranges from $50,000 to $120,000 or more depending on scope, materials, and structural changes required. Projects involving wall removal, custom islands, or high-end solid surface materials fall in the upper range. Mother Hubbard's provides detailed estimates after an in-home design consultation.
What's the difference between a custom island and a semi-custom island?
A semi-custom island uses standard cabinet dimensions and configurations adapted to a specific space. A fully custom island — like the asymmetric design in this Camp Hill project — is designed from scratch around the specific dimensions, traffic flow, and use requirements of the room. Custom islands allow for non-rectangular forms, split countertop surfaces, integrated structural elements like the corbels here, and combinations of function (dining + prep) that standard configurations don't accommodate.
How long does a custom kitchen renovation take from design to completion?
Most custom kitchen projects at Mother Hubbard's move from initial design consultation to completed installation in 12 to 20 weeks. This timeline includes design development (2–4 weeks), cabinet fabrication (6–10 weeks), and installation (1–3 weeks depending on scope). Projects involving structural changes — wall removal, modified load paths — may require additional lead time for permitting and structural work prior to cabinet installation.
